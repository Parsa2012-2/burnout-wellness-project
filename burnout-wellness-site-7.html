01: Understanding the issue

What burnout actually is

Burnout: a state of physical and mental exhaustion caused by long-term stress at work that hasn't been managed. It's a real, recognized workplace problem, not a personal failing and not something a doctor diagnoses on its own.

It matters because it doesn't stay stuck to one bad week. If it's ignored, it can hurt your health, your relationships, and your career. It also costs companies real money through missed work and lost output. Every employee, and every future manager, benefits from learning to spot it early, in themselves and in the people around them.

Part 1

Exhaustion

Feeling drained, physically and emotionally, with energy that doesn't come back over a normal weekend or evening.

Part 2

Feeling distant

Feeling more negative about the job, or far away from work that used to feel meaningful.

Part 3

Feeling less effective

A drop in confidence and how much you get done. Tasks that used to feel manageable start to feel impossible.

02: Causes and who's affected

Where burnout comes from

Burnout rarely comes from one bad day. It builds up when a job asks for more than a person has support to handle.

Common causes

  • Heavy workload that never lets up. This is the single biggest cause reported by Canadian employees
  • Little control over tasks, schedule, or decisions
  • Unclear expectations or priorities that keep shifting
  • Little recognition for effort or results
  • Poor workplace relationships or conflict that never gets resolved
  • Not fitting in with what the company cares about or how it runs

Most affected groups

  • Employees in legal and HR roles report the highest burnout rates of any field
  • Working parents juggling kids or family care with full-time jobs
  • Women and people of color, who report burnout at higher rates than average
  • Managers, who report workplace stress even more often than the staff they lead
  • Healthcare workers and other frontline staff, because of emotional demand and staffing pressure
03: Signs, symptoms and performance impact

How to spot it in yourself or a coworker

Warning signs

  • Feeling tired all the time, even after sleep
  • Getting annoyed easily, feeling negative, or having a short temper
  • Trouble concentrating or making decisions
  • Headaches, muscle tension, or getting sick more often
  • Dreading work, or feeling numb toward it
  • Withdrawing from coworkers and friends

Effect on performance

  • Missed deadlines and slower output
  • More errors and difficulty focusing on detail work
  • Missing more work and showing up late more often
  • Reduced collaboration and patience with others
  • Higher likelihood of quitting or disengaging quietly

05: Solutions and prevention

Managing it, on both sides of the desk

What an employee can do

  • Protect real breaks. Eat lunch away from your desk instead of working through it
  • Set limits on after-hours email and messages
  • Say what you actually need before hitting a breaking point, not after
  • Use vacation and mental health days the way they're meant to be used
  • Build in recovery habits: sleep, movement, and time fully offline
  • Talk to someone early, a manager, HR, or a support line, rather than late

What a workplace can do

  • Keep workloads realistic and staffing matched to demand
  • Give employees real input over how their work gets done
  • Train managers to recognize early warning signs
  • Offer an Employee Assistance Program and make it normal to use
  • Build recognition, not just correction, into everyday feedback
  • Treat burnout prevention as a real budget item, not an afterthought
06: Resources and supports

Where to get help

Support exists at every level. It's free, private, and available whether or not someone has a workplace benefits plan.

Canada.ca Mental Health Helps by connecting anyone in Canada to free mental health tools and counselling options, including support built specifically for workplace stress and burnout. canada.ca/mental-health
Employee Assistance Program (EAP) Helps by giving employees a few free, private counselling sessions through work, so getting support doesn't depend on being able to pay for it. ask HR for the provider name
Canadian Mental Health Association (CMHA) Helps by connecting people to local counselling, workshops, and workplace mental health programs in their own community, in person or online. cmha.ca

In a crisis, Canada's 988 Suicide Crisis Helpline helps immediately, free, by call or text, 24/7.

07: Real-world connections

Why this matters for whatever comes next

Burnout doesn't wait for a specific job title. It shows up in trades, retail, healthcare, tech, and every office in between. That means knowing how to recognize and manage it is a real skill, not just a nice bonus.

Understanding burnout early makes someone a better employee. It means pacing your own workload instead of crashing every few months. It also makes someone a better future leader. A manager who notices when a team is quietly overloaded will keep good people longer than one who waits for a resignation letter to find out. In fields with the highest rates, like legal, HR, and healthcare, this isn't extra knowledge. It's part of doing the job well.

08: Interactive activity

Activity: Blooket

A live Blooket game the whole class plays on their own devices. Everyone joins with a game code, answers the same burnout questions, and the game keeps score in real time, way more energy than a regular quiz.

How this supports workplace wellness

  • Every student plays at once on their own device, low pressure
  • Fast multiple choice keeps a heavy topic feeling light
  • Repeating the facts through a game helps them actually stick
  • The built-in scoreboard keeps energy up without singling anyone out
